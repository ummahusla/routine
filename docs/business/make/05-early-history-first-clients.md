## The Founding Story — Internal Tool Origin

Sources: Arpit Choudhury / Make blog (https://www.make.com/en/blog/how-an-obsession-with-product-experience-made-integromat-a-great-integration-platform); SaaSworthy interview (https://www.saasworthy.com/blog/interview-with-arpit-choudhury-head-of-growth-integromat); EverybodyWiki (https://en.everybodywiki.com/Integromat); CzechStartups interview (https://czechstartups.gov.cz/en/novinky/interview-with-ondrej-gazda-and-patrik-simek-on-an-investment-that-resonates-with-the-startup-world-the-czech-company-integromat-was-acquired-by-the-german-firm-celonis-for-a-price-of-more-than-czk-2/)

**The parent company:** Integrators.cz (Integrators services a.s.) — a Czech systems integrator doing large-scale integrations for banks, insurance companies, and enterprises. The URL is https://www.integrators.cz/.

From the SaaSworthy interview with Arpit Choudhury (Head of Growth, Integromat), June 2021:
> "Integromat was initially built for internal purposes back in 2012 since the parent company had been doing large-scale integrations for banks, insurance companies and other enterprises, with the product being built over a period of four years and launched in 2016."

> "Integromat's parent company, Integrators.cz, had been in the integration space for more than a decade as a systems integrator in the Czech Republic."

From the Arpit Choudhury / Make blog post, "How an Obsession with Product Experience Made Integromat a Great Integration Platform" (originally published November 14, 2019 at https://www.integromat.com/en/blog/2019/11/14/how-an-obsession-with-product-experience-made-integromat-a-great-integration-platform/):

> "Prior to starting the development of Integromat circa 2012, Integromat's cofounders had been orchestrating complex integrations for large enterprises."

> "Integromat's cofounders believed that this tool would set them on a path to becoming the number one Systems Integrator in the Czech Republic, thus Integromat was born in the beautiful city of Prague."

> "Soon after they began developing this internal tool, the I-team came across a SaaS tool that was built on the same principle of removing friction from integrating various apps. While this tool was in its early stages of development and was running a closed beta, SaaS Megastars like Dropbox, Stripe, and several others were changing the rules of the B2B software industry, forever altering the way software is bought and sold."

> "From the start, the goal was to build a product that would always be 10x better than anything that existed or was yet to be built."

From the CzechStartups interview with Gazda and Šimek (November 2020):
> "Integromat is a project whose primary purpose was to save us time, and the idea that we could turn it into a cloud integration platform came only later."

> "One of the biggest roles in Integromat's success was played by the fact that they did not build it up in order to sell it later, and the end-user experience played a significant role in the majority of decision-making processes, taking several years before they felt it was time to show Integromat to the whole world."

## Public Product Launch — 2016

Sources: EverybodyWiki (https://en.everybodywiki.com/Integromat); SaaSworthy interview; multiple cross-references

After approximately four years of internal development starting circa 2012, Integromat was publicly launched in **2016**.

From EverybodyWiki:
> "Integromat was launched in 2016 by co-founders Ondřej Gazda, Patrik Šimek, Michal Toman, Pavel Duras, Tomáš Schel, and Roman Bršlica."

The product launched with:
- Visual "scenario" builder (graphical, node-based workflow canvas — differentiating it sharply from Zapier's linear "Zap" structure)
- Integration with 105+ services at launch (per the August 2016 Hacker News submission)
- Free plan: 1,000 operations per month, 100MB data transfer
- Paid plans starting at $9/month (Basic) and $29/month (Standard)
- No-code visual builder, but with advanced capabilities for developers (HTTP modules, JSON parsing, custom code)

## Pricing Model (Early / Launch)

Sources: Weblytica (https://weblytica.com/how-to/integromat-pricing-plans/); Cloudwards (https://www.cloudwards.net/integromat-pricing/); SaaSworthy

Integromat's pricing was built around **operations** (not tasks or "zaps"), which made it significantly cheaper for power users building complex automations:

- **Free plan:** 1,000 operations/month, 100MB data transfer
- **Basic plan:** $9/month
- **Standard plan:** $29/month
- **Business plan:** Higher tiers for more operations

This operations-based model was a deliberate competitive differentiator against Zapier, which charged per "task" (each action in a Zap counted as a separate task). On Integromat, a filter or router step did not count as an operation.

From the Integromat Medium blog post, "From Zapier to Integromat: What Makes Us Different?" (https://medium.com/integromat/from-zapier-to-integromat-what-makes-us-different-37ebf1e67c85):
> "By charging users per operations, Integromat provides a full-service platform at an overall cheaper price point than Zapier depending on your usage. Integromat's free plan provides 1,000 operations and 100MB data transfer per month."

From a Hacker News user comment (March 2021 Zapier $5B thread):
> [User noted switching from Zapier — "hundreds per month" — to Integromat at $29/month.]

## Competing With Zapier — Positioning and Differentiation

Sources: Integromat Medium blog (https://medium.com/integromat/from-zapier-to-integromat-what-makes-us-different-37ebf1e67c85); Clean Commit comparison (https://cleancommit.io/blog/make-formally-integromat-vs-zapier-a-comprehensive-comparison/); Make blog (https://www.make.com/en/blog/how-an-obsession-with-product-experience-made-integromat-a-great-integration-platform)

Integromat's strategy against Zapier was never direct price competition as a primary message. It was product superiority:

1. **Visual scenario builder** vs. Zapier's linear Zap editor — Integromat's canvas showed branching logic, routers, filters, and data flow visually
2. **Operations pricing** vs. task pricing — cheaper for complex multi-step automations
3. **Advanced modules** — HTTP/REST calls, JSON parsing, XML, SOAP, databases (MySQL, MSSQL, PostgreSQL) baked into the platform
4. **Error handling** — dedicated error handler routes (a feature Zapier lacked for years)
5. **No feature restrictions by plan** — unlike Zapier, multi-step scenarios were available on all plans including free

Patrik Šimek on Make's visual-first approach (https://www.make.com/en/blog/why-make-builds-visual-first):
> "A visual language that democratizes development, makes processes tangible and fosters team collaboration."

Community framing from early days (multiple Hacker News and forum sources):
> "Zapier on steroids" — the most repeated user description of Integromat from 2018 onward

> "Zapier has been a pioneer in the software automation space, [but] many users looking for advanced features are adopting Integromat."

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **WebFetch was blocked** during research — all content comes from search result snippets and indexed text, not full page fetches. Several key primary sources (CzechStartups interview, CzechTheValley interview, Arpit Choudhury blog posts) have confirmed URLs but only partial verbatim text was recoverable.

2. **No named "first customer"** — unlike Zapier's Andrew Warner story, Integromat has not publicly documented or named their first paying customer. The product-led growth model (free tier → upgrade) may mean there was no dramatic "first sale" moment.

3. **"Ondrej Krajicek"** — the name in the research brief does not match any verified founder. The correct CEO is Ondřej Gazda. No record of "Ondrej Krajicek" in connection with Integromat was found across all sources.

4. **Wayback Machine snapshots** — early integromat.com snapshots from 2014–2016 were not accessible via search. Direct Wayback Machine access (web.archive.org/web/*/integromat.com) would be needed.

5. **Hacker News full comment threads** — HN items 12268380, 13168111, and 22788105 are confirmed to exist but only title/preview text was recoverable. Full comment text requires direct page access.

6. **Exact founding date conflict** — sources variously state 2012, 2013, and 2016. Best reconciliation: internal work began 2012 (Šimek's LinkedIn), formal company/s.r.o. incorporation ~2013 (BusinessWire), public product launch 2016 (all sources agree).

7. **Patrik Šimek's founding role** — some early sources attributed co-founding only to Michal Toman, Ondrej Gazda, and Patrik Šimek (three developers), with Duras, Schel, and Bršlica joining as the business formalized around 2016. The exact founding cohort is unclear.
