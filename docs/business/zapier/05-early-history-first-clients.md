## First Paying Customer: Andrew Warner — November 30, 2011

Sources: Zapier blog (https://zapier.com/blog/anatomy-of-sale-or-how-andrew-warner-became-our-first-paying-user/); Taskade history article; First 1000 newsletter (https://read.first1000.co/p/zapier)

**Who:** Andrew Warner, founder of Mixergy (business podcast/interview platform)

**How they found him:**

From the Zapier blog:
> The Zapier team identified customer needs by researching SaaS forums and Google searches for integration requests. Foster discovered Warner asking on Stack Exchange: "How can I auto-import contacts from PayPal into Highrise?" — though the post was 8 months old.

**The cold email:**
Foster sent a low-pressure email acknowledging the question and introducing Zapier's solution. Warner replied that he'd already solved that problem but asked about a Wufoo-to-AWeber integration — something Zapier hadn't yet built.

**The overnight build:**
The co-founders built the Wufoo-AWeber integration overnight to fulfill the request. Wade Foster then notified Warner the integration was complete.

**Warner's reaction:**
> "This is freakin' fantastic."

He immediately asked the price. They quoted $100 for beta access and Warner agreed instantly.

**Payment infrastructure challenge:**
Zapier lacked payment infrastructure, so Foster directed Warner to his personal PayPal account. The team rushed to establish a bank account while Foster set up a PayPal notification Zap. The payment arrived as they entered the bank.

**Warner's tweet (verbatim):**
> "I just paid zapier.com $100 for a product it didn't even launch"

From the listenupih.com article (https://www.listenupih.com/how-to-get-your-first-100-users/):
> Warner's tweet: "I just paid $100 for a product it didn't even launch because @WadeFoster nailed what I was DESPERATE for."

**Date:** November 30, 2011

**Wade Foster on the experience:**
> "We just put him through the worst experience possible and the guy still wants to pay us."
(From Taskade history article)

**Lessons drawn:**
> "We don't need, you know, millions of users. We really just needed like a couple folks to give us a shot" — Wade Foster (Indie Hackers, https://www.indiehackers.com/podcast/018-wade-foster-of-zapier)

## How They Got Their First 100 Customers — The Forum Strategy

Sources: First 1000 newsletter (https://read.first1000.co/p/zapier); Contrary Research; Groove interview (https://www.groovehq.com/blog/zapier-interview-with-wade-foster)

**The core tactic:** Wade Foster manually searched product forums (Dropbox, Salesforce, Evernote, etc.) for integration requests and replied personally.

From the First 1000 newsletter:
> Wade would find forum posts asking for specific integrations and respond with: "Hey, here's how you can do it… here are the API docs to these two services" or offer to build solutions
> Generated 10–15 visits per forum post
> **50% of forum visitors signed up for Beta**

From Wade Foster (Groove interview):
> "I would go to forums where people were posting and asking for integrations... I would just comment in those forums and say 'hey, here's how you can do it.'"

> "You don't need a thousand customers, you just need 1 customer or 10 customers and we were able to go from nothing to something just through this very targeted outreach."

From the Indie Hackers podcast (https://www.indiehackers.com/podcast/018-wade-foster-of-zapier):
> "Just, you know, hanging out in forums where other products exist is like a pretty good way to find ideas"

> "I would actually start commenting in those forums...go to this link and give me your contact information"

> "We didn't need, you know, millions of users. We really just needed like a couple folks to give us a shot"

From the Contrary Research report:
> "those forum links...we'd get 10, 15, 20 visitors from each one, but of those visitors, around 50% would convert and say 'yes, I really want this.'"

## The Paid Beta Model

From the First 1000 newsletter:
> The team "charged users $5–$10 during beta (after testing $100 to $1 pricing iterations)"

> Created self-selecting customer base of those who genuinely needed the product
> Generated higher-quality feedback from invested users

From the Taskade history article:
> Beta Pricing: "$5 for lifetime beta access"

From Wade Foster (Indie Hackers):
> "Pulling a credit card out is like one of the toughest things...we wanted the folks who were gonna be willing to pay"

From Wade Foster (Groove interview):
> "We made people pay a one-time fee to get into our beta... The last thing we wanted was a bunch of tire kickers who are just trying it out for fun."

## The Manual MVP Phase

From the First 1000 newsletter:
> Initial product was entirely manual, not automated
> Wade and co-founders conducted calls with prospects to understand integration needs
> Team would build integrations in the backend and email customers when ready

From Wade Foster (Groove interview):
> "I had a functioning prototype. It wasn't great... I could set it up for them on my end, so that's what I did."

From Wade Foster on customer service during early days:
> "We can't be the best and we can't be the cheapest, but we can definitely care the most, and so from day one, we thought 'let's get on Skype calls.'"

> "I watched hundreds of users set up Zaps, took notes back to engineers. PLG doesn't mean never talking to people—I talked to people constantly."

## Y Combinator Rejection and Reapplication

Source: Zapier blog (https://zapier.com/blog/3-dudes-missouri-built-product-found-paying-customers-and-got-yc/); Changelog podcast; Taskade history

**First application (Winter 2012 batch): REJECTED**

From Bryan Helmig (Changelog):
> "The first time they just flat out rejected us. It was a nice email… Also, a hot tip for folks out there - if you want a good rejection email, go find the Y Combinator one."

**What they did instead of giving up:**

From the Zapier blog:
> The team "found paying customers by 'sleuthing support forums chock full of integration requests' and built features based on actual customer needs."

From Wade Foster (Eric Ries Show):
> "Nothing in our background looked particularly impressive. We doubled down on building instead of dwelling on rejection."

**Second application (Summer 2012 batch): ACCEPTED**

By the time of the S12 application, the founders had:
- A working functional beta
- "A thousand people had paid to get access"
- 10,000 people on a launch waitlist
- December 2011: 10,000 on waitlist, 25 integrations (Contrary Research)

From the Zapier blog on the YC interview:
> The founders wore matching orange Zapier t-shirts
> Paul Graham told them to "stop trying to get to the demo and just answer the questions"
> The interview felt "2 minutes long"
> Acceptance call: "Hey Bryan, its Paul. We loved you guys, we'd like to fund you."

From Bryan Helmig:
> "That summer we just worked; I don't think we slept a whole lot, we'd just work-work-work. We worked 16-hour days pretty regularly."

> "That's not the measuring stick; it's building a successful business, and we always over-indexed on just building a successful business."

## Integration-Page SEO as Early Customer Acquisition

From the First 1000 newsletter and multiple sources:

Early on, Zapier built landing pages for every app-to-app combination:
> "Landing pages for every integration combination boosted SEO. Capitalized on underutilized keyword combinations like '[Company Name] + [Company Name]'"

Wade Foster described the insight:
> "If you're looking for integrations, there's all these different keyword results that you could conceivably rank for, but no one is trying to rank for this stuff." (First Round Review podcast)

This early SEO strategy evolved into the massive programmatic SEO engine described in 06-marketing-zapier.md.

## The Wufoo Connection

Kevin Hale, co-founder of Wufoo (online form builder), was an investor in Zapier's seed round. The connection matters:

1. **Wufoo was one of Zapier's first integrations** — the Andrew Warner / first customer story centered on a Wufoo-to-AWeber connection
2. **Wufoo had been acquired by SurveyMonkey in 2011** — the same year Zapier launched
3. **Kevin Hale later became a YC partner (2013–2020)** and worked directly with Zapier as a YC company
4. **Kevin Hale and Mike Knoop recorded a YC podcast together** (https://www.ycombinator.com/blog/mike-knoop-on-product-and-design-processes-for-remote-teams-with-kevin-hale/)

From Wikipedia:
> Zapier seed investors included "Wufoo co-founder Kevin Hale"

## Wade Foster's Own Account of Early Days

From Wade Foster (First Round Review podcast, https://review.firstround.com/podcast/building-zapier-from-first-principles-contrarian-takes-on-growth-hiring-fundraising-wade-foster-co-founder-ceo/):

> "A lot of company building is literally just like, showing up day after day and doing that for a really long period of time."

> "Pay closer attention to the fundamental things that make your company great: good products, happy customers, and a fun yet hardworking team."

From Wade Foster (Eric Ries Show, https://www.ericriesshow.com/wade-foster-zapier/):

> "If you're going to try and build a company, don't do anything that doesn't matter."

> "Playing it safe is actually the riskiest thing you can do."

> "I'm willing to sacrifice a little bit of revenue for the durability of these customers."

> "I don't even feel like I'm selling. It felt like I was selling lemonade on a hot summer's day." (On early customer conversations)
